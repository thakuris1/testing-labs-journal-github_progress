# CASE-2 — Location Tracking for Workplace Safety
Location tracking can help improve safety, especially for delivery drivers wroking in busy areas. It can also help compaines manage routes better and reduce risks.However, tracking employees all the time can fell like an invasion of privacy,especially if workers do not fully understand how their data is being used.
## Context

CargoMove Logistics manages a large fleet of delivery drivers across London. After several minor safety incidents, management introduced a smartphone‑based location‑tracking app to monitor driver speed, stops, breaks, and routes. The stated purpose is to improve safety and reduce insurance costs.

While drivers were informed of the new system, the consent form was bundled into a long employment update, and many employees report they did not clearly understand how extensively they were being monitored. A few weeks later, managers began calling out drivers for “inefficient routes,” “too many short stops,” and “lingering outside customer locations,” causing discomfort and anxiety.

## The issue

A driver complained to their union that the system collects more data than necessary and feels punitive rather than safety-driven. The union claims the monitoring violates principles of data minimisation and transparency under the GDPR. Meanwhile, management argues that the data also identifies risky behaviours (like speeding) and has already reduced accident rates.

## Decision point

CargoMove must determine whether the tracking system is proportionate, transparent, and lawful — and whether alternative, less intrusive solutions could achieve the same safety goals.

Stakeholders include drivers, unions, management, customers awaiting deliveries, and regulators concerned with worker privacy.
