# CASE-1 — AI Hiring System & Indirect Discrimination
AI hiring systems can be helpful becausevthey save time and make the recueitment process faster. However, it is not always fair to fully rely on them.If the system is trained on past data that already includes bias, it may contiune to treat some candidates unfairyly, such as older applicants or those from certain universities. This kind of indirect discrimination can happen without the company even realizing it.
## Context

BrightHire Ltd., a mid‑sized UK software consultancy, has deployed an AI‑based résumé‑screening system to shortlist candidates for developer roles. The system was trained on 10 years of historical hiring data collected internally. Over the last three recruitment cycles, HR staff noticed that applicants from certain universities and older applicants (aged 45+) were disproportionately ranked lower by the system.
The company relies heavily on the tool because the volume of applications has doubled after switching to remote‑friendly roles. Recruiters now spend significantly less time manually reviewing CVs, which leadership sees as a major efficiency gain.

## The issue

A rejected applicant filed a complaint, arguing that the system unfairly penalised career changers and older workers. An internal audit showed that past hiring practices strongly favoured graduates from a small number of universities and generally younger early‑career applicants. Because the AI model learned from these patterns, it began reproducing them—lowering scores for applicants whose profiles did not match the “historic hire” profile.
HR now asks whether the tool is legally defensible, ethically fair, and socially responsible to keep using without major changes.

## Decision point

BrightHire must decide whether to:
- Suspend or remove the AI tool
- Keep using it but increase oversight
- Retrain/rebuild the model with more diverse data
- Or adopt an entirely different approach

Stakeholders include applicants, HR staff, senior leadership, the technical vendor, and society at large given the wider debate about AI fairness.
